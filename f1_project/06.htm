The first thing a team does prior to the season is testing. These days, a testing schedule for a top Grand Prix team can amount to over fifty days' of solid two-car test work during the year, adding around £1.5m to each team's budget. The exact details of this annual schedule will be debated between the Team Manager and the Chief Engineer and it is then the job of the Test Team Manager to action that plan.

So what is the purpose of all this testing and what do the teams hope to achieve? After all, it rarely seems to affect the "world order" of F1 in that McLaren & Ferrari stay at the top of the tree with everyone else struggling to climb up that slippery pole! Most well-heeled teams will operate with a completely separate Test Team including separate crew, transporters, race cars & engines, equipment, etc. as. Fortunately virtually all testing takes place around Europe so at least the travelling involved is not too much.
There are four primary purposes.
> First and foremost, testing is used for trying out new development parts on the cars before a race meeting to see whether design-office theory equals practice; i.e. will it make the car go faster?
> Secondly, these tests are used for "setting-up" the cars before a Grand Prix; i.e. optimising their performance-related settings for one particular track. This optimisation may well include a full "race simulation" to gather the necessary data referred to in my previous "Strategy" article.
> Thirdly, the team's engine partner will make full use of the tests to carry out development and optimisation work for their engine programme. Their programme will be considered at least as important as that of the chassis development and sometimes whole days of testing will be given over exclusively to solving engine and engine-management-type problems.
> Fourthly, the tests are a useful way for the drivers to hone & practise their skills, try new lines, braking points, etc. without the pressure and career-damaging consequences of making a driving error during a GP weekend.
A modern F1 team will have a design staff of a minimum of thirty-five highly-qualified and motivated designers, all working flat out to dream up ways of making their particular specialist area of the car work more efficiently. Particular importance is given to aerodynamics these days so often there will be revised designs of wings and wing-end plates, barge-boards, floors, airboxes and generally any aero-type tweak that the team can think of.

All these developments will have been intensively tested in a wind tunnel first and details computed for improved lift/drag ratio but the actual "feel" of the car can only be truly realised on the track. There may be some weight-saving parts to be tested - F1 teams continually struggle to reduce the overall weight of the car in order to have more moveable ballast to play with to affect the handling characteristics of the car. There can also be some new parts designed and manufactured to increase the car's reliability which need testing. Most teams will operate a policy of never introducing new parts to the car at a race meeting unless they have been exhaustively tested at pre-event tests first.

The team will gain all the information it needs on the development parts from the on-board telemetry systems which log every conceivable parameter of the car as it circulates around the track. These systems are "state of the art" and require a horde of electronic boffins to operate them and decipher the information. It is true that there is no hiding place on the track for the modern GP driver. If he claims he's flat through a certain corner & his engineer doubts it, the telemetry will reveal all.
Setting-up the cars prior to a Grand Prix will involve a considerable amount of work refining gear-ratios, damper, spring or torsion-bar settings, ride-heights and rake, cambers, casters, wing-angles, diff settings, tyre pressures; in fact virtually anything you care to name on the car will be infinitely adjustable. It is very important for the driver to know how he wants the car to "feel" during this testing.

A further very important aspect to testing these days is the use of a 7-post hydraulic simulator, often referred to as a "shaker rig". This expensive and complex device will be installed in its own room at the team's base and will be capable of simulating any lap of any circuit with a full-scale racing car astride it. It will simulate not only the ride effects of the car on a particular track but also downforce levels front & rear. The rig will have been pre - programmed for a particular track using previously gathered data from the onboard car telemetry systems.

Top teams will set aside a complete race car - identical to those being taken to the GP - to use exclusively on the "shaker rig" over a GP weekend which will be permanently manned with its own specialist technicians and mechanics. That way the race engineers can telephone - or even e-mail from the pitwall - back to the technicians at base while a practise session is in full swing and request them to evaluate a different setting on the "shaker rig" car that maybe they haven't got time to try on the race car itself.

So, ultimately, what is the purpose of all this testing effort? Clearly making the car go faster is the primary aim. The reason it doesn't usually affect the "world order" of F1 is, of course, that all the teams are testing all the time so to progress beyond your early-season form requires your team's testing programme to be even more intense and effective than that of your rivals. And that's not easy!
