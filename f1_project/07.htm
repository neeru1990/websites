Race strategies in Formula 1 look so easy and obvious when you're watching the race from the comfort of your armchair but, on the 'prat-perch' hotseat at the track, there will be engineers from every team sweating hard over what decisions to make to win the race for their man. The execution of a successful strategy will have involved many man-hours of computer toil and then the ability to juggle all the 'strategy balls' in the air to arrive at the most advantageous result..
There is a set of six key 'building blocks' that most teams will go through on a Grand Prix weekend or in testing beforehand to gain the knowledge required to begin setting a race strategy.
These are qualifying position, fuel consumption, tyre-wear, time spent in pitlane, probable weather and ability of the circuit/driver combination to provide overtaking opportunities.

Most teams will arrange to test at the European tracks some 10 days prior to a Grand Prix. This allows them to monitor both fuel consumption, tyre-wear and pit-lane entry/exit times in a very detailed and controlled manner, especially if part of the test includes a full race simulation run. This data is then fed into the computer and this usually points to a very obvious result showing whether a one-stop, two-stop or three-stop strategy is likely to provide the quickest overall race time. Very simply, the more fuel you run in the car, the heavier it will be. Heavier = slower and often more tyre-wear. However more fuel = less pitstops and less time taken in the pitlane as well as the 'In' and 'Out' laps which are usually slower than a normal racing lap.

All the top drivers will endlessly practice the art of approaching the pitlane as fast as they dare in order to cross the pitlane entry line at or just below the regulation 80kph. Additionally, a top driver will often be very impressive indeed on both his 'In' and 'Out' laps whereas a driver with less experience may struggle initially to keep his concentration at this critical moment and be unable to keep pushing hard. Both the driver's race engineer and the driver himself will usually finally decide the race strategy. The race engineer will often work into the early hours of Sunday morning to finalise the best strategy available; although you sometimes wouldn't think so when you see the dire results from some of their efforts.

Above all, the best strategies give the driver/engineer combination the flexibility to adjust their plan as the race progresses. This can be important for several reasons; for example, if you're on pole and your 'light fuel-load strategy' dictated that you streak off into the distance, what happens if you fluff your start and then are held up behind a slower car. Your light fuel load plan is wasted because you can't maximise the speed it gives you - time for a rethink! Track position is also a most important factor in the whole equation - there's clearly no point in being massively fast in the early part of the race, pitting and then exiting the pits behind the Minardis and the Prosts who may hold you up for the next 10 laps. So thinking on your feet is a most important ability for a top race engineer and few equal the mental agility that Ross Brawn seems able to provide every alternate weekend.

A change in weather conditions will alter most team's strategies during a race so accurate weather forecasting is essential. Most teams will subscribe to an e-mailed or faxed daily weather forecasting service for the local area and obtain additional information from the local aerodrome if it's nearby. For those with unlimited budgets, stationing a few strategically placed observers around the track and upwind of the weather with team radios can pay great dividends.
It can often be raining hard on one side of the track but bone-dry in front of the pits! If it rains hard, the lap-times will increase dramatically as the tyre-wear reduces. If you think it will continue to rain, a sound policy is to arrange for your driver(s) to pit immediately for wet tyres and fill the car right up to the brim with fuel. That way, you can hopefully make the end of the race without a further stop, especially if a few pace-car interventions assist your car's fuel economy along the way! It is often surprising how few teams seem brave enough to gamble on this option though. Another often-used strategy may be to shadow the car in front and then attempt to gain an advantage (and track position) by pitting at the same time as your rival but short-filling the car to gain a pit-stop time advantage. This is especially useful if a slower car in front is holding up your car but which still cannot be overtaken in the conventional sense on the track.

The key to success with all these strategies is decisive, logical and rapid decision-making coupled to clear communications between the team's race engineer and both drivers and pit-crew. Occasionally, a team manager's intimate knowledge of the FIA's Sporting Regulations will be severely tested when unexpected circumstances occur. Ferrari pulled a masterstroke at the 1998 British Grand Prix when Schumacher was given a 10-second penalty towards the end of the race. Article 13 of the Sporting Regulations states: "The (Control) Line is a single line that crosses both the Track and the Pitlane". Result - Ferrari bring Schumi into the pits, he crosses the line to win the race and then takes his penalty!
