Modern Formula One races are won or lost in the pits these days. It's a sad reflection on the rulebook that the only way it seems possible to overtake another driver is by your pit-crew producing a faster stop than your rival. As a result, the stops take on tremendous importance for the teams who are continuously looking at ways to decrease their car's stationary time in pitlane. To ensure that this operation passes off with minimum time loss, regular dress rehearsals are held with each mechanic being expected to know his individual role by heart allowing his actions to eventually become automatic reflexes. Most teams will practise these stops early on a raceday morning as well as the preceding Thursday. A successful pit stop involves a minimum of twenty-two people working in total unison with each other.

There will be three mechanics on each corner of the car to change the wheels. The bravest of these three will be kneeling on the ground with an air-powered wheelgun and, before the car has completely stopped, he will have located his gun onto the single, central wheel-nut & be unlocking the nut. Virtually simultaneously another mechanic will have removed the old wheel and the third guy will be locating a new wheel onto the spigots ready for the gunman to torque the wheelnut up to the specified torque of 450ft/lbs.
Once their job is done, the kneeling mechanic will raise his arm high in the air to signify that his work is completed. Two other mechanics will also have cleaned out the radiator ducts on both sides of the car to avoid any unnecessary debris (visor tear-offs, plastic bags, etc.) from causing the engine to overheat through blocked radiators. All of this will be done considerably faster than it took you to read this last paragraph! (roughly 6-7 seconds)

Three further people operate the re-fuelling. The rig operator will operate the 'Dead Man's Handle' on the Intertechnique rig which will be located at the front of the pit garage, one to attach the refuelling nozzle to the car and one to carry the very heavy hose over his shoulder behind the refuelling man. The refuelling hose is not easy to attach to the car as there are male locating lugs in the nozzle end that have to be accurately located to the car's female attachment which means that unless the angle of attachment is exactly correct, the hose won't attach. As with aircraft, the fuel is measured by weight rather than volume so a two-stop race strategy may see around 60kg of fuel gushing into the car's tank in around 4-5 seconds. The rate of flow is 12-13 litres/second.

There will be a further two personnel operating quick-lift jacks at either end of the car. The mechanic wheels the jack into position before applying all his weight to the other end to lift the car. A third person will steady the car while it's off the ground by holding onto the roll-hoop. This is necessary to hold the car still while the wheels are being changed, and to ensure the refuelling nozzle can be attached successfully. Finally there is the Lollipop Man who is responsible for guiding the car into its correct pit and then not releasing the car until the entire process is completed. The Lollipop itself also reminds the driver to keep his foot on the brake ('Brakes On') and when to select 1st gear ('Gear!') There is also a Fireman manning a 25kg Halon fire extinguisher just in case anything should go wrong.

Firstly, it's vital that the driver stops exactly on the marks that have been set for him. This is usually a box marked out in the pitlane with white tape showing him where he must place the car's wheels. The first problem is that, because of today's high cockpit sides, the driver can't see where he is stopping. Secondly, as the driver is coming down pitlane at the regulation 80kph speed-limit (60kph for Monaco), he wants to brake as late as possible to enter his pit in order to lose as little time as possible.
This, coupled with an often slippery surface outside the pit garages, makes it very easy for the car to overshoot the box and, even if this is only by a couple of feet, it can cause major problems as suddenly the entire pit-stop crew are in the wrong position! Even 18 inches short or long in the box area will delay the stop by a couple of seconds as the mechanics re-align their equipment & position to service the car. Worse still, the car can hit either the front jackman or one of the kneeling wheel-gun men, which can cause unpleasant injuries very easily.

Most teams' real fears centre on some kind of fuel-rig failure. These are fortunately very rare but the consequences of equipment failure can be potentially horrendous. One team in the Brazilian GP this year managed to dowse their car & driver with fuel during the race's pit-stop when a sight-tube retaining ring on the fuel-hose nozzle had not been correctly re-assembled during pre-event maintenance and worked loose. Fuel sprayed everywhere and the driver actually had fuel dripping down the inside of his visor! Only a miracle avoided a major and serious fire as, had any of the fuel touched the red-hot exhausts or a brake disc, it would certainly have ignited and caused a major pit-fire.

Teams will practice many different pit-stop scenarios including changing the front and rear wing assemblies, changing failed electronic boxes and steering-wheels, re-starting the engine if the driver has stalled it during the stop and even what to do in the event of the wrong car appearing down the pit-lane; i.e. the team are expecting Coulthard and Hakkinen appears. Each car has its own supply of FIA numbered wheels along with an FIA-appointed observer stationed in the team's garage during the event so putting the wrong wheels on a car will result in certain disqualification at the end of the race, as Arrows found out last year in the French GP.
