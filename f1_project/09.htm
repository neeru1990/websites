How often have you heard drivers and commentators talking about dirty air? A strange term it must be said, yet it refers to a very real problem Grand Prix drivers are faced with and must take much of the blame for the lack of action we see on the track. It refers to the effect of the disturbance the car in front has on the air. This causes a problem because Grand Prix cars depend to a large degree on their wings to help them grip around corners. Like inverted aeroplane wings, instead of making the car fly, they push it down onto the road allowing it to corner faster.

Airflow is a strange thing, though, and it can be easily upset. The flow over the car's wings needs to be smooth and undisturbed, the kind of flow you get when driving through still, undisturbed air. However, when a racing car cuts through the air, it creates a mass of turbulence commonly referred to as dirty air. Any car following, will find that this disturbance has a dramatic effect on the efficiency of its wings, producing less downforce and less grip.
The net result is when one car gets close enough to try and overtake the car in front, it is suddenly at a huge disadvantage. Less grip makes it slower round the corners, and that means slower down the straights too. Above all, it makes overtaking massively more difficult. This was not always the case, however, due to another phenomenon associated with following another racing car. In addition to upsetting your downforce, being the following car has an advantage. As it plunges through the air, the leading car creates a hole that quickly closes behind it. Get close enough behind the leader and you are not pushing as much air. With less work to do, the car accelerates better and will ultimately go faster catching the car in front.

This is slipstreaming and has been known about ever since racing cars started going reasonably quickly. In the days before the dependence on wings to improve grip, this put would put the car behind at an advantage. On a fast circuit like Monza or Hockenheim, the following car could accelerate up behind the leader, ducking out at the last minute and use the extra momentum to slingshot past.Often races would go on like this from start to finish with the disadvantaged leader being continually replaced by another. Obviously it would be great to return to these heady days but it is not so simple.

Technology cannot be uninvented, and while rules can outlaw certain aspects of it, the underlying principle will still be exploited somehow. Having said that, there are moves afoot to frame technical regulations that will put the following vehicle at less of a disadvantage. In the US, ChampCar organisers came up with a device to reduce downforce and hence cornering speeds. It is a simple vertical panel across the back of a rear wing, but it was found to have an interesting side effect. Suddenly following cars were at less of a disadvantage and the track action perked up accordingly.

Whether anything like that will come to pass in Formula 1 is in the hands of the organisers. Alternatively, they could simply ban wings, but how would you replace all that valuable advertising space?
