The logistics of moving a Formula One army to a different venue and country every fortnight represents no small achievement for every one of the current teams, no matter how well-funded or not they may be.

Each team will send around forty-five people to a race who form the operating core of the Race Team. The advance guard consisting of four "truckies" aboard an articulated Transporter and a rigid-bodied Transporter will leave their base sometime over the preceding weekend and arrive at the Grand prix track on the Monday night before a race. These trucks will carry all of the garage support equipment including the banner system for decorating the sides and back of the garage and a complete overhead gantry system to support lighting, air, power and computer connections for all three race cars.

They will also carry some of the bulkier items such as the two Intertechnique refuelling rigs, the "prat-perches", team scooters and around forty sets of wheel-rims and tyre-warming blankets. The truckies' first job on arriving will be to clean and paint the pit garage floors in order that the setting-up of the garage banners, air and electric supplies can begin unhindered on Tuesday. The complete set-up will take a further two days of hard slog and results in a complete internal team garage being constructed within the circuit's own pits. This early crew will also oversee the installation of up to four ISDN lines in the garage, which are then used to carry the team's e-mail and data traffic both back to base and around the world. Many teams will have technicians and engineers back at base scanning all the telemetry from the cars as it's happening. In fact, Ferrari have their own satellite dish and permanent link in order to be certain of confidential and reliable communications between the track & Maranello.

Another major job for this advance guard will be to set up the team's "Prat-Perch" stands (great name even though I did sit on it - don't know who thought of it!) along the pit-rail. These contraptions will usually seat around eight personnel (2 x Race Engineers, 2 x Data Engineers, Chief Engineer, Team Manager, Team Owner & Engine Manufacturer's representative) and will become the team's nerve centre during the race & qualifying. A bank of fifteen TV monitors will provide access to every TV picture being captured around the circuit along with data channels displaying lap-times, sector times, cars on circuit, weather and any messages from Race Control. The Engineers will also be able to monitor real time car data via an infrared data link beaming the signals from the pit garage to the prat-perch. The crew will also have the job of feeding the team from the Wednesday through to the Sunday night. Feeding a hungry crew of around forty-five with three hearty meals a day from a mobile kitchen is no small task and some of the team's cooks are legendary figures around the paddock!

Two days later on the Wednesday preceding the Grand Prix, the majority of the team will fly in to join their colleagues at the track. A further two Transporters with four more Truckies will arrive at the circuit during the afternoon. One of these will carry the three cars and a huge selection of spare parts and consumables. This truck will also house a complete machining centre comprising lathe, mill, drill, linisher, borescope and full welding facilities.

The other transporter will serve primarily as a mobile office suite, with one office for the Race Engineers and Drivers and a further office for the Team Manager and his assistant. It may also be used for additional storage carrying including housing the massive two 20metre high radio antennae which are erected by compressed air and stand high above the transporters' roofs to transmit & receive voice & data signals to the cars while travelling around the circuit. Both the teams' transporters will carry 90kva generators so that they are completely self-contained for power in case the circuit's own power supply becomes over-loaded and crashes. Quite a normal occurrence at some circuits unfortunately! An average track day for a mechanic will involve leaving the team's hotel around 6.00am in time to grab a quick breakfast at the track before continuing work on the car. It can be rare for these guys to be back at their hotel much before midnight on each of the three "running" days so, by Sunday night, you will have a lot of very tired mechanics propping up the bar!

Packing up is a very rapid affair in comparison to setting-up. The majority of the team will be aiming to fly home on the last available Sunday night flight and then a skeleton crew is left behind to finish packing up the garage banners and gantry on the Monday. The cars will be back at base from most European GPs by Tuesday but, by the following Monday, those transporters must be rolling again towards the next race. Such a schedule leaves little time for testing, development and updates to the cars, let alone holidays and time-off for the team members!
